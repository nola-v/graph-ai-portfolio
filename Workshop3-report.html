1. Executive Summary

This report examines 400 data analyst job postings to understand the relationships between median salary estimates, minimum years of experience required, and demand for programming languages (R and/or Python).

Key Findings:

  • The overall median salary across all postings is $68,000, with a wide range from $33,500 to $150,000.
  • Minimum years of experience shows almost no linear relationship with salary (correlation = 0.051), suggesting that other factors—such as industry, company size, location, or specialized skills—are stronger salary drivers.
  • Python is the most in-demand programming language among postings that specify a language requirement (62 postings), followed by roles requiring both Python and R (68 postings).
  • Roles requiring both Python and R command the highest average salary (~$75,900), though Python-only and "Both" categories share the same median ($69,000).
  • R-only roles are rare (n = 15) and exhibit the lowest median salary ($61,500), but also the least variability.
  • The majority of postings (63.8%) do not explicitly require either R or Python, yet their salary distribution is comparable to language-specific roles.

3. Analysis 1: Salary vs. Minimum Years of Experience

Methodology

A scatter plot was generated using the 310 postings with valid numeric experience requirements. A linear trend line was fitted to assess the direction and strength of the relationship.

Findings

  • Correlation (Pearson r): 0.051 — This indicates a negligible positive linear relationship.
  • Trend slope: Approximately +$573 per additional year of required experience.
  • High-salary outliers ($120,000+) appear at nearly every experience level from 0 to 8 years, suggesting that experience alone does not gate the highest compensation tiers.
  • The densest cluster of postings falls between 2–5 years of experience and $50,000–$85,000 in salary.

Interpretation

The scatter plot shows a weak positive relationship between the minimum years of experience required and the median salary estimate. The dashed trend line indicates that salary tends to increase slightly as experience requirements rise, but the spread of data points around the trend is wide. Many postings cluster in the 0–5-year experience range, with salaries varying widely from approximately USD 35,000 to USD 130,000 at any given experience level. A small number of high-experience postings (10–15 years of experience) show elevated but inconsistent salaries. Limitation: 90 of 400 postings had missing experience data and were excluded from this plot. The small effective sample size for high-experience roles (n < 20) means the trend at the upper end should be interpreted with caution.

4. Analysis 2: Salary by Data Language

Methodology

A box plot was generated using all 400 postings to compare the distribution of median salary estimates across the four standardised programming language categories: R, Python, Both, and Neither. Box plots display the median, interquartile range (IQR), and outliers for each group, making them well-suited for comparing salary spread and central tendency across categories.

Findings

  • Both (R and Python): Highest mean salary (~$75,900) and a median of $69,000, with the widest spread, reflecting diverse role types that require dual-language proficiency.
  • Python only: Median salary of $69,000, closely matching the "Both" categories, suggesting strong and consistent market demand for Python skills.
  • Neither: Median salary of $68,000, comparable to language-specific categories, indicating that many well-compensated roles do not explicitly advertise a language requirement.
  • R only: Lowest median salary ($61,500) and the least variability across the group, though this category contains only 15 postings, limiting the reliability of this estimate.
  • Outliers above $120,000 are present across all four categories, confirming that high-compensation roles exist regardless of language requirement.

Interpretation

The box plot reveals modest differences in salary distributions across language categories. Roles requiring both R and Python have a slightly higher mean salary than the other groups, which may reflect the additional technical breadth expected of candidates. However, the overlap in salary ranges across all four categories is substantial, and the differences in medians are small, spanning only $7,500 from the highest (Both, $69,000) to the lowest (R, $61,500). This suggests that programming language requirements alone are a weak predictor of salary, and that other factors such as industry, company size, and seniority are likely more influential. Limitation: The R-only category is substantially smaller (n = 15) than the others; its salary distribution should be treated as indicative only, not generalied to the broader market.

5. Personal Reflection

I need to be honest about what I thought before I started this. In Fiji, where I have worked in health, the pay system is pretty straightforward. You put in the years; you move up the scale. A nurse with five years gets more than one with two. A doctor with ten years is on a different band entirely. So, when I first looked at this salary data, I just assumed the same thing would happen. More experience equals more money. It turns out that assumption does not really hold here, and I am still processing that.

Analysis 1

The correlation was 0.051. I actually ran it twice because I thought I had made a mistake. In the health surveys I have worked on back home, a number that low would mean there is basically no relationship. And that is exactly what the scatter plot showed. The thing that really got me was seeing salaries over $120,000 for jobs that wanted zero years of experience. Zero. In Fiji, that would never happen unless you were a specialist coming back from overseas with years of training. Here it is just... normal? That messed with my head a bit.

I think the difference is that Fiji's health system operates under public-sector pay bands. Everything is structured. This dataset is just... messy. Employers seem to throw out "minimum years" as a rough estimate rather than an actual rule tied to salary. I kept looking at the plot and thinking, is this flexibility or is it just chaos? I still do not really know.

I also want to mention the 90 postings I had to drop because they did not list experience. That bothered me more than I expected. In Fiji, every Ministry of Health job ad clearly lists the requirements. When I saw that nearly a quarter of this data had nothing, I started wondering what kind of employers those were. Small companies that do not bother with formal ads? I keep thinking that if I had those 90 postings, my whole story might change. I will never know, and that annoys me.

The high experience end was another issue. Less than 20 postings. I do a lot of survey work back home, and if someone tried to tell me anything definitive based on 20 people, I would laugh. But the trend line still draws itself across that space like it knows what is happening. It does not. I had to keep reminding myself not to pretend the data was saying something it was not.

Analysis 2

This one was personal. I have started learning R to do some data analysis in health. I only started learning Python, so I was hoping this analysis would show that the time I spent on it was worth it financially.

Then I saw that "Neither" matched Python basically exactly. Same median. That was a real moment for me. I sat there thinking about all the nights I have spent learning R, fixing do-files, and merging datasets from our national health surveys. Was that about making myself more money, or was it about being able to do my job without waiting for some overseas consultant? It is probably the second one. And that is fine, but it is not what I was measuring here.

What I am taking from this

I think the main thing is that I need to stop assuming the Fijian health system is a template for how other labour markets work. It is structured, predictable, and transparent. This market is none of those things. Experience does not pay the way I thought. Programming languages do not separate people the way I thought. Everything is more tangled.

If I do more of this, I want to split the data by actual job title and industry first. "Data analyst" and "data scientist" are not the same thing, just like "nurse" and "doctor" are not the same thing. I treated them like they were, and that was lazy.

This project was good for me. It was uncomfortable. The data did not agree with my assumptions, and I had to sit with that. I am used to health data where things mostly behave the way you expect. This did not. I am leaving with more questions than answers, which I guess is the point of actually doing analysis rather than just confirming what you already believe.
